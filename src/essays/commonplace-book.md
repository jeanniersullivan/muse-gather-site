I keep a plain notebook on the corner of the desk, and into it goes any sentence that makes me stop. Not the clever ones, necessarily — the true ones. The ones that name a thing I'd felt but never managed to hold still long enough to look at.

The practice is older than any of us. Renaissance students kept them; so did Woolf, so did Didion. A commonplace book is not a diary and not a scrapbook. It is a record of your attention — proof of where your mind actually went, as opposed to where you meant to send it.

> "You only get one life. The least you can do is notice it."

After a year, the book becomes a strange kind of self-portrait. You see the seasons you were anxious, the months you were tender, the writers who were quietly rebuilding your sentences from the inside. It is the most honest thing I own.

## How to begin

Start small. One line a day. Don't organize it. Let it be a little wild, like a garden you tend but never fully control — front porch, wildflowers, curiosity. The value isn't in the system; it's in the returning.

Some months you'll fill three pages a week. Others, a single line will have to carry you. Both are the practice. What you're building, slowly, is a habit of noticing — and noticing, it turns out, is most of what it means to be awake to your own life.
